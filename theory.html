Signal representation and orthogonality

Theory

Vectors and dot products

To understand orthogonality of signals, we recall the familiar notion of vectors in 3D space and their representation. If i \mathbf{i} i, j \mathbf{j} j, and k \mathbf{k} k denote the unit vectors along the three perpendicular axes of the reference coordinate system, any arbitrary vector v \mathbf{v} v can be represented as

v=ai+bj+ck \mathbf{v} = a \mathbf{i} + b \mathbf{j} + c \mathbf{k} v=ai+bj+ck

In the Cartesian coordinate system, the unit vectors i,j, \mathbf{i}, \mathbf{j}, i,j, and k \mathbf{k} k correspond to the points (1,0,0), (0,1,0), and (0,0,1) respectively. The dot product of any two vectors v1=(a1,b1,c1) \mathbf{v}_1 = (a_1,b_1,c_1) v1​=(a1​,b1​,c1​) and v2=(a2,b2,c2) \mathbf{v}_2 = (a_2,b_2,c_2) v2​=(a2​,b2​,c2​) is defined as

⟨v1,v2⟩=v1⋅v2=a1a2+b1b2+c1c2 \langle\mathbf{v}_1,\mathbf{v}_2\rangle = \mathbf{v}_1 \cdot \mathbf{v}_2 = a_1a_2 + b_1b_2 + c_1c_2 ⟨v1​,v2​⟩=v1​⋅v2​=a1​a2​+b1​b2​+c1​c2​

By this definition, we can see that any two vectors from the set [i,j,k] [\mathbf{i}, \mathbf{j}, \mathbf{k}] [i,j,k] have zero dot product. In general, any two perpendicular vectors will have zero dot product and are said to be orthogonal to each other. Additionally, when orthogonal unit vectors are used as basis to represent vectors, the coefficients can be easily found. For example, when [i,j,k] [\mathbf{i}, \mathbf{j}, \mathbf{k}] [i,j,k] are used as basis,

a=v⋅i,b=v⋅j,c=v⋅k a = \mathbf{v} \cdot \mathbf{i}, \quad b = \mathbf{v} \cdot \mathbf{j}, \quad c = \mathbf{v} \cdot \mathbf{k} a=v⋅i,b=v⋅j,c=v⋅k

Dot product of signals and orthogonality

In an analogous way, we can generalize and define the notion of dot product (and hence orthogonality) for signals. Signals can be thought of as vectors with infinite dimension. For continuous-time signals x1(t) \mathbf{x}_1 (t) x1​(t) and x2(t) \mathbf{x}_2 (t) x2​(t) , their dot product is defined as

⟨x1(t),x2(t)⟩ =∫t=−∞t=∞x1(t)x2(t)dt \langle\mathbf{x}_1 (t),\mathbf{x}_2 (t)\rangle~ = \int_{t=-\infty}^{t=\infty} \mathbf{x}_1 (t) \mathbf{x}_2 (t) dt ⟨x1​(t),x2​(t)⟩ =∫t=−∞t=∞​x1​(t)x2​(t)dt

Two signals x1(t) \mathbf{x}_1 (t) x1​(t) and x2(t) \mathbf{x}_2 (t) x2​(t) are said to be orthogonal if their dot product, as defined above, is zero, i.e., ⟨x1(t),x2(t)⟩ =0 \langle\mathbf{x}_1 (t),\mathbf{x}_2 (t)\rangle ~= 0 ⟨x1​(t),x2​(t)⟩ =0. In other words, the product signal y(t)=x1(t)x2(t) \mathbf{y}(t) = \mathbf{x}_1 (t) \mathbf{x}_2 (t) y(t)=x1​(t)x2​(t) has equal amount of positive and negative area.

For periodic signals x1(t) \mathbf{x}_1 (t) x1​(t) and x2(t) \mathbf{x}_2 (t) x2​(t) with same period T, their orthogonality can be verified by computing the integral of the product within a single period T. Thus, periodic signals x1(t) \mathbf{x}_1 (t) x1​(t) and x2(t) \mathbf{x}_2 (t) x2​(t) with period T are orthogonal if

⟨x1(t),x2(t)⟩ =∫t=0t=Tx1(t)x2(t)dt=0 \langle\mathbf{x}_1 (t),\mathbf{x}_2 (t)\rangle~ = \int_{t=0}^{t=T} \mathbf{x}_1 (t) \mathbf{x}_2 (t) dt = 0 ⟨x1​(t),x2​(t)⟩ =∫t=0t=T​x1​(t)x2​(t)dt=0

The notion of dot product and orthogonality can be extended to complex signals. If x1(t) \mathbf{x}_1 (t) x1​(t) and x2(t) \mathbf{x}_2 (t) x2​(t) are periodic complex-valued signals, their dot product is defined as

⟨x1(t),x2(t)⟩ =∫t=−∞t=∞x1∗(t)x2(t)dt \langle\mathbf{x}_1 (t),\mathbf{x}_2 (t)\rangle ~= \int_{t=-\infty}^{t=\infty} \mathbf{x}_1^{*}(t) \mathbf{x}_2 (t) dt ⟨x1​(t),x2​(t)⟩ =∫t=−∞t=∞​x1∗​(t)x2​(t)dt

where x1∗(t) \mathbf{x}_1^{*}(t) x1∗​(t) denotes complex conjugate of x1(t) \mathbf{x}_1 (t) x1​(t). For example, the signals ejπt e^{j \pi t} ejπt and ej2πt e^{j 2\pi t} ej2πt are orthogonal signals.

Discrete-time signals

In a similar fashion, for discrete-time signals, dot product can be defined as

⟨x1[n],x2[n]⟩ =∑n=−∞n=∞x1[n]x2[n] \langle\mathbf{x}_1 [n],\mathbf{x}_2 [n]\rangle ~= \sum_{n=-\infty}^{n=\infty} \mathbf{x}_1 [n] \mathbf{x}_2 [n] ⟨x1​[n],x2​[n]⟩ =n=−∞∑n=∞​x1​[n]x2​[n]

For finite N N N-length signals, dot product can be defined as

⟨x1[n],x2[n]⟩ =∑n=0n=N−1x1[n]x2[n] \langle\mathbf{x}_1 [n],\mathbf{x}_2 [n]\rangle ~= \sum_{n=0}^{n=N-1} \mathbf{x}_1 [n] \mathbf{x}_2 [n] ⟨x1​[n],x2​[n]⟩ =n=0∑n=N−1​x1​[n]x2​[n]

Fourier Series

As in vectors, orthogonal signals are extensively used in representation of signals. Very often, they form the building blocks for various ways of signal representation. For example, in Fourier series analysis, a periodic signal is represented using sinusoids as follows:

x(t)=a0+akcos⁡(2πkf0t)+bksin⁡(2πkf0t), \mathbf{x}(t) = a_0 + a_k \cos(2\pi k f_0 t) + b_k \sin(2\pi k f_0 t),x(t)=a0​+ak​cos(2πkf0​t)+bk​sin(2πkf0​t),

where T=1f0 T = \frac{1}{f_0} T=f0​1​ is the period of x(t) \mathbf{x}(t) x(t). The set of signals {1,cos⁡(2πkf0t),sin⁡(2πkf0t)} \{1, \cos(2\pi k f_0 t), \sin(2\pi k f_0 t)\} {1,cos(2πkf0​t),sin(2πkf0​t)} are the building blocks in Fourier series representation of periodic signals, where 1 1 1 denotes the constant signal. All the signals in this set have the common period of T T T. We can verify that any two signals in this set are orthogonal. Above is the trigonometric Fourier series representation.

Haar Wavelet

Orthogonality is a recurring feature of many other signal representations, for example wavelet decomposition. In wavelet theory, a mother wavelet function is used to generate the building blocks by performing scaling and translation of this function. The set of functions (wavelets) thus generated form a pairwise orthogonal set of signals. These building blocks can be used to represent any continuous real function with finite support.

Advantages of orthogonality

Finding the coefficients becomes easy when a signal is decomposed into a set of orthogonal signals. Consider the trigonometric Fourier series representation given above. We can find the coefficients {a0,ak,bk} \{a_0, a_k, b_k\} {a0​,ak​,bk​} by computing the dot product of the given periodic signal x(t) \mathbf{x}(t) x(t) with the signal associated with each of these components. Thus

a0 =1π∫−ππx(t) dt a_0 ~= \frac{1}{\pi} \int_{-\pi}^{\pi} \mathbf{x}(t)~ dt a0​ =π1​∫−ππ​x(t) dt
